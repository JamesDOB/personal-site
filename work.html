College Admissions Workshop
Photo / Screenshot coming soon
The Problem

Schoolhouse.world had 160K+ learners on platform but no structured support for one of the highest-stakes moments in a student's life: college admissions. Students were asking questions in community spaces with no consistent, quality guidance. The gap was real and the demand was there — nobody had validated it yet.

The Approach

I built the College Admissions Workshop Program from scratch — no playbook, no precedent. That meant recruiting and vetting tutors, designing the program structure, setting quality standards, and running the first cohort end-to-end.

  • Recruited and onboarded 30 tutors with relevant experience
  • Designed the session format, tutor guidelines, and learner intake process
  • Ran feedback loops throughout to identify friction and improve in real time
  • Tracked engagement and satisfaction to build the case for adoption
The Outcome

The program hit 95% learner satisfaction and drove 2x marketplace engagement compared to baseline. It validated real demand and was adopted as an official Schoolhouse.world program offering — from 0 to institutional in one cohort.

Trust & Safety · Community Infrastructure
Custom Slackbot & Moderation Infrastructure
The Problem

A Slack workspace of 20,000+ tutors and volunteers is a lot to manage. Policy violations were happening in real time, high-risk interactions were hard to surface, and the people keeping it safe were me and a small handful of volunteers doing it manually. It wasn’t scalable. It wasn’t sustainable.

The Approach

I owned this end-to-end. Claude served as a collaborative thought partner throughout — working through architecture, pressure-testing logic, and helping translate technical tradeoffs into plain language. I also brought in a trusted group of active volunteers to test the bot in real conditions and provide feedback, which meaningfully shaped V2.

The Build

I built V1 from scratch — scoping which channels to monitor, what categories of risk to detect, how to handle each severity level, and what to say to users when something was flagged. After running it live and incorporating feedback from the volunteer testing group, I identified core areas for improvement.

V2 is a lot smarter. Every risk category got pressure-tested against feedback from volunteers. The highest-risk stuff goes straight to a dedicated alert channel for me and my team to review, and lower-severity violations get a warm nudge — corrective, not punitive — before anything escalates. I also moved it off my local machine and onto Railway, so it runs 24/7 without me needing to be at my laptop — necessary for a global community that never really sleeps.

The Result

What was manual, volunteer-dependent, and only active when I was online is now automated and running 24/7 across the highest-risk channels. Every infraction can be tied to a user’s account, creating a record that informs moderation decisions over time. Behavioral nudges happen in real-time, catching things early and helping users course-correct in the moment. Now I can turn my Slack alerts off when I go to bed and sleep soundly.

What’s Next

I’m exploring expansions — powered by Claude — to capture user feedback and sentiment in real-time, categorizing it automatically for product and community review. The long-term vision is pushing small bug fixes like copy changes directly to the org’s GitHub repo as PRs — a non-technical operator closing the loop between community signal and product response, autonomously.
